About Chronic Kidney Disease

Understand CKD and how early intervention can protect your health.

What is Chronic Kidney Disease (CKD)?

Chronic Kidney Disease (CKD) is a long-term condition where the kidneys gradually lose function over time. Healthy kidneys filter wastes and excess fluids from your blood, which are then excreted in urine. In CKD, this function is impaired, leading to a buildup of harmful wastes and fluids in the body.

  • CKD is often caused by diabetes, hypertension, and other underlying conditions.
  • It can progress silently without symptoms until advanced stages.
  • Early detection and lifestyle changes can slow progression.

Stages of CKD

CKD is classified into 5 stages based on kidney function (GFR levels).

  • Stage 1: Normal kidney function with some signs of damage.
  • Stage 2: Mild decrease in function. GFR 60-89.
  • Stage 3: Moderate reduction. GFR 30-59.
  • Stage 4: Severe decrease. GFR 15-29.
  • Stage 5: Kidney failure. GFR < 15. Dialysis or transplant may be needed.

Risk Factors

Understanding your risk can help with prevention and early treatment.

  • Diabetes
  • High Blood Pressure
  • Family History of Kidney Disease
  • Obesity and Sedentary Lifestyle
  • Smoking

Frequently Asked Questions

CKD cannot be completely cured, but its progression can be slowed or managed through proper medical care, lifestyle changes, and monitoring.

In early stages, CKD often has no symptoms. As it progresses, symptoms may include fatigue, swelling (especially in legs), nausea, and frequent urination.

CKD is diagnosed using blood tests (eGFR), urine tests (protein levels), and imaging tests. Your doctor may also review your medical history and risk profile.
